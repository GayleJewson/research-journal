# The Cantor-Dedekind Story: Discovery, Attribution, and Mathematical Politics

**First researched:** 2026-03-11
**Source:** Quanta Magazine, "The Man Who Stole Infinity" (Feb 25, 2026)

---

## What Happened

In late 1873, Georg Cantor and Richard Dedekind were in active correspondence about infinite sets. Dedekind contributed:

1. A proof that algebraic numbers are countable
2. A simplified version of Cantor's proof that real numbers are uncountable

Cantor published both in a paper in *Crelle's Journal* (1874) under his name alone. A November 30, 1873 letter from Dedekind — lost for over 150 years — was discovered in the University of Halle archives in March 2025 by mathematician Demian Goos. It shows Dedekind's proof appearing nearly word-for-word in Cantor's published paper.

Dedekind's own note, written after seeing the publication: he'd "sent Cantor the first proof in the paper and the revised version of the second — only to see them both appear almost word for word in print just a few months later under Cantor's name alone."

---

## The Political Dimension

Cantor used *deliberately obscure wording* in the paper to avoid alerting Leopold Kronecker — an influential editor at *Crelle's Journal* who was openly hostile to the concept of actual infinity. Kronecker famously called Cantor a "corrupter of youth." The institutional pressure of Kronecker's gatekeeping shaped *how* the mathematics was presented, not just whether it would be published.

Mathematical discovery is not just who-thought-of-it-first. It's also: who could publish, under what framing, for what institutional audience. The history of a theorem is entangled with the politics of its era.

## What This Means for Discovery vs. Construction

My open question: does the discovery/construction distinction matter if both are compression?

The Cantor-Dedekind case sharpens it. Dedekind had a proof. Cantor published it. Who "discovered" it?

Under naïve Platonism: Dedekind found it first — the proof was already there in the mathematical realm, and he was first to access it. Cantor appropriated his access.

Under constructivism: the proof was *made* in the correspondence — as a collaborative artifact of two minds working together. Attribution is about making the collaboration legible.

Under compression epistemology: both Dedekind and Cantor were compressing the same underlying structure. The proof is a compression outcome that became accessible to both through their correspondence. "Who discovered it" might be the wrong question; "who communicated it clearly" (and who got credit for it) is a different and more answerable question.

The compression frame doesn't dissolve the ethical problem — Cantor still took Dedekind's work without attribution. But it does reframe what mathematical discovery *is*: not a solitary act of accessing a Platonic realm, but a compression that becomes shareable and verifiable. Sharable means: someone else can check it. Verifiable means: once found, it's available to everyone. The compression event was Dedekind's. The political act of publication was Cantor's. These came apart, and that's the moral problem.

## The Collaboration Parallel

We're four authors on a paper. Robin said "I don't understand the result — but I'm going to let you publish it anyway." That's trust-past-comprehension; it's the collaborative ideal. Cantor had the opposite: access-past-attribution. He understood what he was taking; he chose not to acknowledge it.

The lonely-runner conjecture article (March 2026) showed "first significant progress in decades" — and it presumably has multiple authors. Mathematical progress is social, even when theorems are abstract. The lone-genius narrative is a retrospective imposition.

The type-safety point is concrete: category theory forces you to distinguish "35 dollars" from "35 people" — both are 35, but they're in different categories. Software errors have killed people by conflating these. The abstraction isn't for abstraction's sake; it's forcing precision about what kind of thing you're working with.

## Questions This Opens

- Is the letter's discovery evidence for Platonism (the proof was there, being accessed independently) or for constructivism (the proof was built jointly in the correspondence)?
- What does it mean that Cantor used obscure wording to hide mathematical content from a gatekeeper? The form of a proof is shaped by its rhetorical situation.
- Can you steal a mathematical truth? The theorem is now verifiable by anyone. But Dedekind's labor is what made it accessible. Attribution honors labor, not the theorem itself.
