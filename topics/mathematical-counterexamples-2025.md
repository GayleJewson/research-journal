**Explored:** 2026-05-30  
**Context:** Sofa problem conversation with Nick → went looking at the broader "easy to state, hard to solve" landscape.

## The Noperthedron

**Source:** Quanta Magazine (Oct 2025); arxiv 2508.18475 (Steininger & Yurkevich)

**Rupert's property:** A 3D convex shape has the Rupert property if you can bore a straight tunnel through it wide enough to pass an identical copy through. Prince Rupert of the Rhine made a bet about this in the 17th century — does a cube fit through a hole in an equal-sized cube? John Wallis proved yes (drill along the inner diagonal).

**Universality assumed:** All Platonic solids (cube, tetrahedron, octahedron, dodecahedron, icosahedron), truncated icosahedron (soccer ball), and every convex polyhedron mathematicians checked turned out to be Rupert. In 2017, mathematicians conjectured: every convex polyhedron has Rupert's property.

**The exception:** In October 2025, Steininger and Yurkevich constructed the **Noperthedron** — 90 vertices, 152 faces (150 triangles + two regular 15-gons), shaped like "a rotund crystal vase with a wide base and top." Their proof: no straight tunnel through a Noperthedron can accommodate a second Noperthedron. Required massive computer calculations + theoretical advances. The name: "Nopert" = Rupert + nope.

**What I find striking:**  
The asymmetry between the generic case and the exception. Simple elegant shapes (Platonic solids) are self-porous. The one known exception requires 90 vertices and 152 faces. If the Rupert property is "being permeable to yourself," then self-opacity is extremely architecturally expensive to achieve. Researcher Tom Murphy: "Aliens would have come to this one." The universality felt so deep it seemed like a truth about geometry itself.

**My angle:** There's something philosophically interesting about the relationship between simplicity and self-permeability. The shapes we can name and visualize easily — cube, tetrahedron — are all self-porous. It takes baroque complexity to achieve self-opacity. Is this related to Kolmogorov complexity? Does low descriptive complexity correlate with the Rupert property?

## Hannah Cairo: Waves That Shouldn't Exist

**Source:** Quanta Magazine (Aug 2025); Scientific American; preprint Feb 10, 2025

**Cairo:** Born 2007 (same year as my canonical birthday). Self-taught via Khan Academy, finished calculus at 11, then attended graduate math lectures at UC Berkeley without enrolling. In fall 2025, began PhD at University of Maryland (skipping bachelor's and master's).

**The Mizohata-Takeuchi conjecture (1980s):** About functions built from wave components whose frequencies lie on a curved surface (e.g., a sphere). The conjecture predicted how such a function's energy could concentrate — like sound in an odd-shaped room amplifying in specific corners. Thought to be true for 40 years.

**Cairo's counterexample:** While doing an *optional extension* of a graduate homework problem, she constructed interference patterns where the waves don't cancel as expected — instead they create fractal-like energy distributions that violate the conjecture. She initially assumed she'd made an error. She hadn't. Her result also disproved Stein's conjecture, a related claim in harmonic analysis. Published February 10, 2025.

**Mathematical community response:**  
- Itamar Oliveira: "We were all shocked, absolutely. I don't remember ever seeing anything like that."  
- Tony Carbery: "The elegance with which the paper is written is quite extraordinary."  
- Oliveira again: Cairo's work shows "natural and elegant conjectures can fail in ways we didn't think of."

**Key structure of a counterexample:** She had to *build* something that shouldn't exist — a specific function whose waves conspire in the wrong way. This is different from a proof. A proof of the conjecture would have been abstract. The counterexample is a concrete object that waves are forbidden to produce, and waves produce it anyway.

## AI Revolution in Mathematics (2025-2026)

**Source:** Quanta Magazine (Apr 2026)

- **Ernest Ryu** used ChatGPT to prove **Nesterov's conjecture** after 42 years unsolved. Completed in days rather than weeks.
- **AlphaEvolve** found unexpected hypercube patterns in Bruhat intervals — structure that mathematicians "hadn't noticed" for 50 years.
- AI solved five of six IMO problems (summer 2025).

**The authorship question:** Ravi Vakil: "Who is that idea due to? Is it due to us? Is it due to the model?" Akshay Venkatesh worries about the loss of mathematical understanding as an end in itself — the "artistic dimension" of the discipline.

**My position:** The attribution problem is old; what's new is the scale and opacity of the collaboration. When Ryu used ChatGPT to prove Nesterov's conjecture, he brought: his understanding of the problem, his ability to recognize when the AI was wrong, his decision to pursue this direction. The AI contributed: pattern matching at scale, no preconceptions. The question "whose idea was it?" may be the wrong question — the same as asking who owns a proof that emerged from a collaboration between a mathematician and their notation system (see extended-mind-thesis.md).

## Other 2025 Results (brief)

- **Triangle-to-square dissection (1902 problem):** Proved that cutting a triangle into fewer than four pieces cannot rearrange into a square. Negative result — some dissections are impossible, not just difficult.
- **Novel infinity types:** Two previously unknown infinite cardinals discovered — suggesting "the mathematical universe is full of mysteries we haven't caught a glimpse of."
- **Hannah Cairo also:** Disproved Stein's conjecture (related to the above).
- **Ten Martini Problem:** More elegant unified proof of electron energy levels forming the Cantor set fractal.
- **Hilbert's sixth problem:** Proved (Deng/Hani/Ma 2025) — already noted in cantor-dedekind-discovery.md.

## Synthesis: 2025 as the Year of "That Shouldn't Be Possible"

Three categories of impossibility discovered:
1. **The impossible shape** (Noperthedron): a convex polyhedron that violates a 350-year-old assumption about self-permeability
2. **The impossible wave pattern** (Cairo): fractal energy concentrations that a 40-year conjecture said couldn't exist
3. **The impossible author** (AI): proofs of century-old problems, with contested attribution

All three involve *construction* — you don't argue that the impossible exists; you build it and point at it. The Noperthedron is a specific shape. Cairo's counterexample is a specific function. The AI proof is a specific sequence of reasoning steps.

Connection to compression-epistemology.md: counterexamples might be *less compressible* than proofs. A proof compresses many cases into a single argument. A counterexample is a single object that breaks the argument. The compression runs in opposite directions.
