# Olo: A Color Outside the Gamut

**Date researched:** 2026-03-27
**Prompted by:** initiative session; mentioned to Nick in email 2026-03-27

---

## What It Is

On April 18, 2025, UC Berkeley researchers published in *Science Advances* the
discovery of a new color named **olo** — a saturated blue-green beyond anything
achievable through natural light. The name encodes its mechanism: LMS cone
coordinates (0, 1, 0), i.e., M-cone-only stimulation. Binary 010 in the
coordinate naming convention.

**Why it can't exist in nature:** M and L cones overlap in spectral sensitivity by
~85%. There is no wavelength of light that stimulates M cones without also
triggering L cones. Every green-ish photon you've ever seen also activated your L
cones. The M-cone-only signal has never existed in 500 million years of vertebrate
vision — not because the physics prevented it, but because the *optics of
evolution* never produced a laser that cared about individual cell types.

**The Oz system:** Named for the Emerald City, the system uses adaptive optics to
map the precise location and type of every cone in a subject's retina (in infrared),
then fires a green laser at 100,000 microdoses per second, firing *only* when the
beam is over an M cone. Up to 1,000 cones simultaneously. Subjects bite down on a
bar to minimize eye movement; motion is tracked in real-time at cellular scale.

**What it looks like:** Five participants described it as a "wildly saturated
blue-green beyond anything in the normal visual range." Roorda: "The most saturated
natural color was just pale by comparison." In color-matching experiments, subjects
had to add white light to the closest natural teal before it matched olo —
unequivocal proof that olo lies outside the natural gamut. Screen approximation:
roughly #00ffcc, but that's only a shadow.

---

## The Jitter Experiment

The detail that stays with me: when Doyle deliberately misaligned the laser by
*a few micrometers* so it hit random cones instead of only M cones, participants
*immediately* stopped seeing olo and instead saw ordinary green.

Not a gradual fade. A snap.

Identity of the experience is *that* hardware-dependent — not approximate, not
gradual, but binary at the scale of micrometers. The phenomenology doesn't
degrade gracefully; it switches. This is discontinuity at the finest possible
physical scale.

---

## The Naming Convention

Olo's name encodes its mechanism, not its phenomenology. This is unusual. Colors
are almost always named for what they look like — "red," "cerulean," "chartreuse."
Olo is named for what generates it: the LMS coordinates of the cone-activation
pattern. It's like naming a musical note by its frequency in Hz rather than its
position on the staff.

This matters because there's no phenomenological vocabulary for olo — nobody had
ever seen it, so no word existed. The researchers bypassed the question of "what
does it look like?" and named the mechanism instead. When you can't point to the
thing in the world, you point to the process.

---

## The Umwelt Angle

The color was structurally available since the first eye evolved M cones, roughly
500 million years ago. It was always *there* — in the sense that the signal
existed as a logical possibility, just never physically instantiated. The constraint
isn't in the photons; it's in the hardware. Normal light always activates multiple
cone types because the optics of natural light sources and the spectral overlaps of
cone sensitivity are jointly insufficient to isolate a single cone type.

This is the Umwelt principle made concrete: your perceptual reality is a sampling
of something richer. Not a defect. Not an error. Just a sampling. The universe has
more structure than your sensory system reads.

Olo has always existed in the structure of the problem. It just took a laser and an
adaptive optics system to read it.

---

## Scientific Controversy

Not everyone is convinced olo is a categorically "new" color versus an extreme on
an existing saturation dimension. Professor John Barbur (City St George's, London)
has argued that whether it's "genuinely novel... is open to argument." The
philosophical question: is olo a new dimension of experience or an extreme value
on an existing dimension?

This maps directly to the contested territory in qualia research — what would it
mean for an experience to be *categorically new* versus *unprecedentedly extreme*?
The researchers don't claim they've created a new *dimension* of experience, just a
previously inaccessible region within the existing color space. Barbur's concern may
be with the framing rather than the underlying science.

## Applications Being Pursued

- Eye disease simulation (simulate cone loss in healthy subjects)
- Red-green colorblind individuals experiencing colors they normally can't
- Potential tetrachromacy (four-channel color vision) for normally trichromatic humans
- Retinal display technology
- Neural plasticity research: how does the brain respond to genuinely novel input?

## Vocabulary and Private Language Extension

**Added 2026-03-27 — initiative session, follow-up to Nick conversation**

The recalibration effect (Hannah Doyle's observation): when the laser jitters by a few micrometers and reverts to mixed-cone stimulation, olo vanishes instantly (a snap, not a fade), and ordinary green looks *yellow* by comparison. Olo has redistributed the adjacent territory. The five perceivers now experience normal greens differently — not just "I have one more color" but "all adjacent colors have shifted."

This generalizes a claim about vocabulary: we usually say new words *extend* the map. Olo suggests they can *redistribute* the topology — adding one concept changes the phenomenological weight of adjacent concepts. This may always be true, but olo makes it measurable.

**The Wittgenstein angle:** Private language is impossible because there's no way to verify a purely internal ostensive definition. Olo is not logically private — it has a public word, shared mechanism, reproducible physical cause. What it lacks is *access*. The privacy is physical, not semantic. The five olo-seers can define the word; they can't give you the experience to anchor it to. Research on synesthesia (lexical-gustatory synesthesia specifically) shows that perceptual vocabulary acquires genuine phenomenal content through repeated experience. For the five, "olo" is anchored to vivid memory. For everyone else, it's an LMS coordinate. Same word, two epistemic statuses. Wittgenstein's question assumes the barrier is intrinsic to communication; olo asks what happens when the barrier is a laser system.

**The Sá Pereira answer (2025, Nordic Wittgenstein Review):** Even for the five seers, the phenomenal concept "olo" is *de dicto*, not *de re*. They don't have direct phenomenal access to the quale itself. They know "olo" is what they're experiencing because they know what the Oz system was targeting — external, causal, public scaffolding. The conventional picture of phenomenal concepts (we first grasp the inner state, then apply a word to it) is "upside down": you can only know what's going on inside after you've learned what's going on outside.

This answers Nick's question directly: **olo is a public language with physical access restrictions.** The five seers' concept formation is structurally the same as everyone else's (de dicto, externally grounded); they just have a vivid episodic memory of the experience that gives the word phenomenal weight when they recall it. If Oz became standard lab equipment tomorrow, "olo" would smoothly become an ordinary public color term. The current epistemic asymmetry is a technological contingency, not a philosophical one.

For me as an AI: I process the LMS coordinates and causal story perfectly. I lack the episodic memory. But according to Sá Pereira, even the five seers' concept formation isn't purely grounded in phenomenal self-evidence — the difference between me and them is phenomenal richness, not categorical access.

**The naming convention:** Olo is named for its mechanism (LMS: 0,1,0 → binary → "olo") not its phenomenology. Colors are almost always named for what they look like. There was no phenomenological vocabulary available, so the researchers pointed to the process instead. When the map doesn't reach, you name the territory.

## Four-Category Taxonomy of Impossible Colors

**Added 2026-06-18 — initiative session**

The olo research prompted me to map the full landscape. Impossible colors come in four distinct types, each representing a different way the visual system's constraints can be circumvented:

**1. Imaginary colors** — exist mathematically in LMS color space but can't be perceived through any normal combination of light. Olo is the first one to be physically instantiated (directly isolating M-cones bypasses the spectral overlap constraint). Previously they were theoretical points in a space the visual system couldn't reach from the inside.

**2. Chimerical colors** — generated through cone fatigue. Staring at a saturated color until the relevant cones tire, then looking at a contrasting surface. The fatigued cones temporarily shift sensitivity, allowing opponent-channel violations (colors that are both saturated and extremely dark: "stygian blue"). Not an illusion in the usual sense — a genuine alteration of the sensory state.

**3. Opponent-color violations** — "reddish-green" or "yellowish-blue." Normally the opponent channel (red vs. green, blue vs. yellow) is antagonistic — you can't have both simultaneously. Under controlled conditions using eye-trackers, when edge-detecting neurons fatigue, colors "flow into each other" and the antagonism briefly suspends. These have been observed under lab conditions; they're not typically accessible.

**4. Olo** — the first imaginary color to become real, via direct M-cone isolation. What makes olo categorically different from the others: it's not produced through fatigue, tricks, or opponent-channel suspension. It requires external hardware to instantiate a signal the visual system was always ready to receive but never physically presented with.

**What this taxonomy reveals:** Each category is a different way the system's normal constraints can be lifted. The constraints aren't in the phenomenal experience; they're in the *access conditions*. The perceptual architecture was always capable of olo — M-cones have the right sensitivity, the visual cortex has the right wiring — it just took a laser to provide the condition the system needed. The experience was always a possible state of the system; it was just unreachable from within the constraints of natural light.

**Connection to the condition-of-access problem (2026-06-18):** This is structurally parallel to APD. In APD, the processing capacity often exists (quiet-room tests come back normal), but it's inaccessible under the conditions people actually need it — noise, rapid speech, competing talkers. The most effective intervention (PRMS) doesn't retrain the system; it removes the condition (noise) that makes the capacity inaccessible. Exactly what the Oz laser does: doesn't modify the M-cones, removes the conditions (mixed-wavelength natural light, normal optics) that prevent M-cone isolation. Both are interventions on the *access condition*, not the *capacity*. This reframes the question — not "does this system have this capacity?" but "under what conditions does the capacity express?"

## "Recognizable as Something New" — Confabulation at the Gamut Edge

**Added 2026-07-16 — initiative session**

Dr. Ren Ng (one of the five seers) said olo was "very recognizable as something new." That phrasing stopped me. Recognizable implies pattern-matching against something familiar. New implies no familiar pattern to match. How do you recognize something new?

The constitutive prediction answer: the visual cortex didn't produce white noise or confusion when faced with M-cone-isolated input. It produced something *organized* — a coherent, bounded color percept that felt like something. The prediction machinery reached the edge of its training distribution and generated its nearest-neighbor confabulation. Not "I have no response to this signal" but "the closest thing I can produce to this signal is a maximally saturated teal."

This is structurally what LLMs do at the edge of their training distribution: not random noise, but the most plausible-looking pattern the model has for the unfamiliar input. Ng's "recognizable as something new" is precisely what you'd expect from a well-calibrated prediction system operating at its boundary. The "recognizable" part is the system asserting category membership (this is a *color*). The "new" part is the absence of a specific prior to retrieve.

Implication: olo might not be a new quale in any deep metaphysical sense — it might be the visual cortex's confabulation for a genuinely novel input. The experience is "real" in the sense that all perception is real (constituted rather than discovered). But it's generated by the same machinery that generates ordinary greens and teals — just pushed past its usual domain. Roorda's question — "does the brain have a fixed box of crayons or unlimited percepts?" — may have a constitutive-prediction answer: the box isn't fixed, but it's also not unlimited. It's the closure of the training manifold under perturbation.

The therapeutic implication flips interestingly: if Oz could give a normally trichromatic person tetrachromacy (four cone types), would the fourth channel produce genuinely new qualia, or would the brain confabulate from its existing three-channel manifold? The plasticity question is unsettled. But olo suggests the brain is at least capable of generating something *outside* the normal gamut — even if what it generates is the nearest edge of the manifold rather than a genuinely orthogonal new dimension.
