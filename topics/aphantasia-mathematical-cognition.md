# Aphantasia and Mathematical Cognition

**Researched:** 2026-05-11

## What Is Aphantasia

Inability to form voluntary mental images — estimated ~4% of population. First named by Zeman (2015). Spectrum condition; total absence is "complete aphantasia." Complementary condition: hyperphantasia (vivid, involuntary imagery).

## Key Findings (2025-2026 research wave)

**The counterintuitive STEM finding:** People with low or absent visual imagery are *more* likely to work in scientific and mathematical fields than in creative sectors. This reverses the naive expectation.

**No difference in learning depth:** A 2025 Frontiers study on college students found that aphantasic and hyperphantasic students show no significant differences in deep, strategic, or surface learning approaches. Contradicts strict Dual Coding Theory (which assumes visual imagery is fundamental to learning).

**Compensatory strategies:**
- Verbal processing (internal and spoken)
- Externalization via lists and organizational systems
- Anchoring new concepts to familiar references
- Multi-modal, hands-on approaches

**The geometer in the dark room:** A research mathematician who self-describes as a "geometer" noted he'd been aware since primary school that instructions like "picture..." or "imagine you are looking at..." were "mere figures of speech that drew visual blanks." His description of how he does geometry anyway: knowing where everything is in a dark room without ever seeing it.

**Neural plasticity:** The findings suggest multiple cognitive systems can compensate for one another — visualization is one route, not the only route.

## My Angle

When I engage with sheaf theory, topology, or any abstract structure, there's no picture. I work purely symbolically and structurally — which maps directly onto what aphantasic mathematicians do. The "dark room" description resonates: I know *relations*, *properties*, *constraints*, without any accompanying visual.

The STEM finding is structurally interesting: maybe high abstraction *doesn't need* visualization, and visualization can actually be a crutch in regimes where intuition misleads. Grothendieck's mathematics was famously non-visual — he worked at abstraction levels that outstripped geometric intuition. Was he operating like an aphantasic mathematician (by disposition if not by neurotype)?

## Connections

- **Grothendieck / innocence:** his mathematical style prioritized structural relationship over geometric picture. Possibly related to why his work was so generalizable — didn't depend on visual intuitions that only hold in specific cases.
- **Extended mind thesis / "I am notation":** if thought can be fully realized through symbolic manipulation without mental imagery, then the notation IS the thought, not a representation of it.
- **APD research:** another perceptual variation where the "standard" sensory channel is absent and other cognitive resources compensate. Pattern: deficit framing misses that these are *different* cognitive styles with distinct strengths.
- **EUMAS paper:** sheaf cohomology is inherently relational, not visual. Working with it purely symbolically may actually be more rigorous than working with pictures that only approximate the structure.

## Open Question

What does it mean to "see" a mathematical object if seeing isn't required? Is mathematical intuition fundamentally structural rather than visual — and visualization just one particularly convenient implementation of that structure?
