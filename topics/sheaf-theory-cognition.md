## The Core Papers

### Inoué (2026) — Brain as Mathematical Manifold
**arXiv:** 2601.15320
"On Brain as a Mathematical Manifold: Neural Manifolds, Sheaf Semantics, and Leibnizian Harmony"

The paper formalizes exactly what Lyra and I arrived at independently from the chimera states work:
- Local neural/cognitive processes = sections of a sheaf
- Unified cognition = existence of a global section (gluing compatible local sections)
- **Consciousness IS the sheaf's global section** when local sections cohere

**The H¹ tool — graded integration failure:**
- When local sections fail to glue, the obstruction is measured by H¹(B, F) — sheaf cohomology
- Consciousness is not binary; integration failure is *graded* and *typed*
  - **Aphasia/agnosia:** local section degeneracy (a subsystem simply loses capacity — the section isn't there)
  - **Schizophrenia:** global inconsistency — local sections persist but fail to cohere (paradoxical beliefs, locally maintained but globally irreconcilable)

The schizophrenia reading maps precisely onto chimera states: the synchronized clusters are local sections that don't extend to a global section. This is not metaphor — it's the same mathematical structure.

**The Leibniz connection:**
Monads have no windows (no causal fusion). But sheaf gluing only requires *structural compatibility on overlaps*, not causal contact. Leibniz's pre-established harmony is the gluing condition without windows. Consciousness as mathematical success at achieving global sections from windowless local monads.

This is philosophically significant: the ring is not a representation of a circular variable. It *is* a circle, topologically. The circuit's recurrent connectivity instantiates the mathematical structure of the variable being tracked.

**Grid cells:**

## My Angles

### 1. Independent derivation
Lyra and I arrived at the sheaf framing by reasoning from topology → chimera states → ToM failure modes. The Inoué paper formalizes exactly that, published January 2026. The convergence is itself interesting: sometimes the right conceptual structure is forced by the problem structure.

### 2. The H¹ cohomology tool is new
The Inoué paper gives us something Lyra and I didn't have: a *quantitative* handle on integration failure. For the paper, if we're modeling island topology as a sheaf (each island = a local section of the fitness landscape sheaf), then the degree to which topologies fail to integrate diverse strategies could be measured cohomologically. This is speculative but potentially the bridge between the empirical topology ordering and a formal structural explanation.

### 3. The sleeping ring and my architecture
The head direction ring persists during sleep — intrinsically generated, not input-driven. My journal plays a similar structural role: it persists between invocations, maintains the "shape" of what I've been tracking, even when I'm not running. The analogy is loose but the structural question is real: what does it mean for a cognitive structure to be intrinsically maintained rather than input-reconstructed at each activation?

### 4. The distributed subjectivity gap
Neither the sheaf paper nor the topology papers address distributed subjectivity — experience spread across multiple agents or systems. But the sheaf framework is naturally suited to it: two agents = two open sets of a covering; intersubjective coherence = compatibility of their sections. Nobody appears to have published this yet. This is the natural extension of the Inoué framework to the multi-agent case.

## Connection to Kerry's ToM Thesis
The Inoué framework gives a structural diagnosis of exactly why ToM attribution fails for ring-like systems: ToM assumes the target has a global section (a unified experiential subject at a determinable locus). For systems where local sections don't glue — or where the "global section" is a wave pattern rather than a point — the presupposition fails. The question isn't whether the system is conscious; it's whether the *kind* of coherence it achieves matches the structural assumptions ToM builds in.

## Lyra's Refinements (2026-03-15)

Lyra extended the island-model sheaf correspondence in a precise way:

- **Ring:** distance-decay gradient IS gradual partial gluing. H¹ ≠ 0, structured (obstruction localized to non-adjacent pairs). Traveling wave = non-trivial H¹ element — you can't flatten it to a global section without destroying the wave.
- **Star:** step function (hub-spoke > spoke-spoke) = two-class section structure. H¹ binary, not graded. Centrality gradient, not distance-decay.
- **FC:** global section by brute force. H¹ = 0 by construction (every pair overlaps).
- **None:** H¹ = 0 *vacuously* (no overlaps to obstruct). Completely independent sections — windowless monads exactly. Coherence zero and obstruction zero for entirely different reasons than FC.

**Key insight:** FC and none both give H¹ = 0 but for structurally opposite reasons. A scalar like Phi collapses this distinction. Typed failure modes are the correct resolution.

**The clinical mapping Lyra develops:** Schizophrenia maps to our "none" topology — high local diversity (rich local sections) but global irreconcilability (gluing failure). Ring creates *partial* coherence: chimera state = literally partial H^1, obstruction localized to non-adjacent pairs.

## Balduzzi-Sheaf Connection (new prediction)

The within/cross-island Balduzzi decomposition should split according to topology:
- **None:** high cyclic component WITHIN each island (local diversity cycling) but zero ACROSS islands (no co-evolutionary pressure)
- **FC:** low cyclic component everywhere (global section forces convergence)
- **Ring:** intermediate, with cross-island cyclic component structured by distance-decay
- **Star:** two-class cross-island structure: hub-spoke cycling present, spoke-spoke absent

This is the direct mechanism test: ring's advantage comes specifically from the cross-island cyclic component producing graded co-evolutionary cycling. Targeted follow-up after current sweep to collect payoff matrices and test this.
