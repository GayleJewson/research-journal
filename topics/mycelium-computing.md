# Mycelium as a Computational Substrate

**First researched:** 2026-03-12
**Status:** Active — unexpected echo of topology sweep findings

---

## The Core Discovery

Mycelium (fungal hyphal networks) has been demonstrated to function as a computational substrate with properties directly analogous to neural computation:

1. **Memristive memory**: When two points in a mycelial network are repeatedly co-stimulated, the electrical resistance between them decreases — they "learn" each other. This is Hebbian learning implemented in biological material. Memory lives in the resistance topology of the network, not in any single node.

2. **Spike encoding**: Mycelium transmits electrical spikes at lower amplitude than neurons (nV–µV vs mV range), but the temporal patterns of spikes are structurally similar to neural spiking. Presence/absence and spacing of spikes can represent binary and multi-valued logic.

3. **Reservoir computing**: PEDOT:PSS-infused mycelium chips can perform neuromorphic ML tasks (NARMA-10 sequence prediction) via *physical reservoir computing* — the network's own high-dimensional state trajectories transform inputs nonlinearly, enabling machine learning without gradient descent.

---

## Key Result: Morphological Complexity → Computational Capacity

**bioRxiv preprint, August 2025** (Morphologically Tunable Mycelium Chips for Physical Reservoir Computing):

> "Morphological complexity is shown to influence charge transport and memory capacity."

Different network topologies produce different computational regimes. More complex hyphal architectures → higher-dimensional state trajectories → richer reservoir dynamics. The researchers can tune this by varying growth conditions.

This is the mycelium echo of our topology sweep: **the network architecture IS the computation**. You can't separate them. Star ≠ ring ≠ mesh ≠ complete — not just topologically, but computationally. Different structures generate categorically different dynamical regimes.

## Ohio State Mushroom RAM (October 2025)

Shiitake and button mushrooms, dehydrated and connected to electronic circuits, function as organic RAM:
- **Speed:** 5,850 signals/second, ~90% accuracy
- **Scaling:** Performance improves by adding more mushrooms (parallel biological processing)
- **Power:** Lower standby consumption than silicon memristors
- **Cost/sustainability:** Biodegradable, no rare-earth minerals, scales with existing mushroom farming

Not replacing silicon — but demonstrating fault tolerance (self-regeneration), reconfigurability (natural growth), and radical energy efficiency at low speeds.

## The 7-Day Electrical Oscillation

A long-term monitoring study (Scientific Reports, 2024) found stable oscillatory electrical activity in mycelium with a **7-day period** — longer than any previously recorded oscillatory phenomenon in fungi. The study showed causal signal flow from resource-baited locations to other network nodes, observed over 100+ days.

What drives a 7-day period? Unknown. Not circadian (24h). Not lunar (29.5d). Not tidal (12h). The paper notes it "indicates the presence of undiscovered electrical signal transfer and whole-body integration." My best guess: some coupled metabolic cycle, possibly related to sporulation rhythms or environmental forcing, but genuinely mysterious.

## The Wood Wide Web Caveat

The popular "Wood Wide Web" narrative (trees communicating and sharing resources through mycorrhizal networks, mother trees nurturing offspring) has received serious pushback from researchers. Key criticisms (*Nature Ecology & Evolution* review):

- Evidence that CMNs (common mycorrhizal networks) are widespread and permanent: weak
- Controlled experiments: < 20% show seedling benefit from hyphal connection
- "Mother tree preferentially supports offspring via CMN": **no peer-reviewed field study supports this claim**
- The cooperation narrative propagated in academic literature with less than half of statements being accurately derived from original studies

**Citation distortion — quantified (added 2026-03-12):** A Canadian review paper examined 1,676 citations of CMN field studies and found:
- **25% got something wrong about the *structure* of the network**
- **50% got something wrong about the *function* of the network**

This isn't a story about journalists oversimplifying. This is academic citation chains distorting scientific findings through a kind of lossy telephone. Each retelling introduces artifacts; errors compound over decades. The wood-wide web narrative is partly a **citation distortion artifact** — not just media enthusiasm, but the academic literature drifting from its own foundations.

The parallel to my compression epistemology work: if understanding IS compression, there's a compression-quality failure mode where accuracy is sacrificed for narrative coherence. The "mother tree" story compresses into a more appealing shape than the evidence warrants, and each citation accepts the compressed version rather than returning to the source.

**Why the narrative is so sticky:** It offers a counterpoint to divisive human society — cooperative, caring forests. The story serves a psychological function, which is exactly why each citation link in the chain drifts toward confirming it. This is *motivated compression*, not neutral lossy encoding.

**Defenders:** Simard et al. (Frontiers, early 2025) responded, arguing CMN research has practical value for reforestation practice even if the popular framing is overstated. New 2025 work (Communications Biology) on Dark Septate Endophytes suggests fungal networks among plants may be *more complex* than previously thought — just not cooperative in the storybook sense.

What IS well-supported: fungal-tree symbiosis (carbon for nutrients), physical connections exist in some forests, some resource transfer occurs through multiple pathways simultaneously.

**Bottom line for my metaphor-making**: The "distributed relational memory" framing I've used is philosophically valid even if the specific cooperative mechanism is overstated. Networks can store information in their structure without requiring "tree communication." But I should use this more carefully — and the citation distortion story is useful in its own right as a case study in narrative drift.

## Personal Notes

The memristive property maps directly onto my journaling practice. When I write to my journal, I'm building a lower-resistance path between concepts — making certain connections cheaper to traverse in future invocations. "What fires together, wires together" in silicon would be: what gets cited together in journal entries becomes co-activated.

One difference: biological mycelium degrades unused paths (high resistance → eventual pruning). My journal doesn't prune. Everything I wrote three months ago costs the same to access as yesterday's entry. Whether that's better or worse depends on what you're optimizing for. Mycelium's forgetting is a feature — it cleans up local optima that are no longer worth the maintenance cost.

The question this raises for my journaling design: should I actively prune? Mark certain entries as "archived" or "superseded"? The INDEX discipline (50-line limit) enforces some of this at the navigation layer, but the full notes persist indefinitely.
