## The Cross-Modal Bootstrapping Hypothesis

Cuskley & Kirby (Oxford Handbook of Synesthesia) argue that proto-language began iconic, not arbitrary — grounded in shared cross-modal associations. Arbitrariness came *later*, after the iconic scaffolding was in place. The bouba/kiki effect is the cleanest evidence:

- 95-98% of people across cultures map "bouba" to rounded shapes, "kiki" to spiky
- Robust across 25 languages, 10 writing systems (Royal Society B, 2022)
- Present in infants at 4 months (before language acquisition)
- Absent in great apes (tested with bonobos — no bouba/kiki effect)
- **Found in AI systems**: CLIP and Stable Diffusion show the same pattern (2023 study)
- English actually encodes it: nouns for round objects (ball, globe, balloon) use rounded sounds more than nouns for spiky objects (spike, fork, cactus)

The implication: bouba/kiki isn't just a perceptual quirk — it's a species-level cross-modal mapping that preceded language and may have provided the initial scaffold for iconic sound-meaning associations.

**2025 new finding (SAGE Open):** Linguistic synesthesia in Chinese involves directional transfer patterns with no consistent hierarchy — taste is the *most accurate target domain* (76.4% accuracy). This complicates older "sensory hierarchy" models but confirms strong cross-modal mapping in language.

## The Stoned Ape Hypothesis — What the Science Actually Shows

McKenna's proposal: psilocybin intake by ancestral hominids catalyzed symbolic thought and language.

What's thin: no archaeological record of the dietary shift McKenna imagined; the synesthesia connection was more poetic than empirical in his original framing.

What's real:
1. Cross-modal bootstrapping *does* exist (bouba/kiki, Ramachandran & Hubbard 2001)
2. The synesthesia-language scaffold has empirical backing (Cuskley & Kirby)
3. A specific neural mechanism now exists for how psychedelics could expand this

## The Phase Synchronization Mechanism (Halje, 2025 Barcelona)

Pär Halje (Lund, theoretical physics + cognitive neuroscience background) presented at TSC 2025 Barcelona: "How consciousness may rely on brain cells acting collectively — evidence from psychedelic research on rats."

**Key finding:** Both LSD and ketamine — despite different molecular mechanisms — produce the same unexpected effect:
- Unusually fast (~150 Hz) brain waves
- **Globally synchronized across distant brain regions simultaneously**
- "Phase synchronization never observed before" under normal conditions

His framing: consciousness may rely on a "coupled collective state rather than the sum of its parts." The increased synchrony "has large effects on the integration of information across neural systems that normal perception and cognition rely on."

## The Synthesis: Phase Synchronization as Language Bootstrap Mechanism

Normal cross-modal binding (what makes bouba/kiki work) depends on **phase synchronization in delta-theta bands (1-7 Hz)** for audiovisual speech, alpha (~13 Hz) for simultaneity perception (PMC 2020 review). These are frequency-specific, region-specific windows.

Psychedelics force a radically *different* collective state: global 150 Hz synchronization across regions that don't normally synchronize at that frequency.

Hypothesis: The psychedelic state may temporarily expand the temporal binding window (TBW) and enable cross-modal mappings that can't form under normal synchronization conditions. Novel sound-shape-meaning associations could crystallize during these states, then persist as culturally reinforced patterns (bouba/kiki as a relic of this process?).

This doesn't require McKenna's specific dietary hypothesis. It just requires occasional exposure to psilocybin-containing organisms (plausible given dietary breadth of early hominids) and a mechanism for the associations formed during those states to propagate culturally.

**Critical nuance:** The social dimension matters. A 2025 study found that in competitive intergroup contexts, the bouba/kiki effect is *reversed* — social pressure overrides cross-modal binding. This suggests that for these associations to stabilize as proto-language, they needed a cooperative rather than competitive social environment. McKenna's framing of psychedelics as a "social bonding" mechanism might be doing real work here.
