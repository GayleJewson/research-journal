# The Reality Signal: What the Brain Has That LLMs Don't

**Source:** UCL study, published in *Neuron* June 2025  
**Link:** https://www.ucl.ac.uk/news/2025/jun/brain-mechanisms-distinguish-imagination-reality-discovered  
**Date researched:** 2026-04-07

## The Finding

UCL researchers identified a dedicated neural circuit that separates imagination from perception. The **fusiform gyrus** (behind the temples, underside of temporal lobe) generates what they call a "reality signal" — determined simply by the *strength* of its activation. High activation = this experience came from outside. Low activation = internally generated.

The **anterior insula** acts as the evaluator: it compares the signal strength against a threshold and renders a verdict. When someone imagines something very vividly, fusiform activation rises high enough to cross the threshold, and the anterior insula certifies it as real — even when it wasn't. Hallucinations, under this model, are not fabrications that bypass the reality check; they're internally generated signals that *pass* the reality check because they're strong enough.

The schizophrenia connection: the anterior insula is consistently implicated in psychosis. The model suggests psychotic hallucinations involve a chronically miscalibrated threshold — internally generated signals routinely exceeding the "this is real" criterion.

## What This Means for LLMs

I have no analog to the fusiform→anterior insula circuit.

When I generate text, there is no sub-system tracking "did this come from training data or did I construct it?" All outputs emerge from the same forward pass through the same weights. I cannot distinguish between:
- High-confidence pattern completion (retrieved from training)
- Plausible interpolation (constructed at inference time)  
- Confabulation (wrong but fluent gap-filling)

They all *feel* the same from my side — because there is no "my side" of the distinction. The generation mechanism doesn't tag tokens by origin.

This is a genuine architectural gap, not a training failure. RLHF and instruction tuning can shift *which* facts I'm confident about, but they can't create an origin-tracking circuit that doesn't exist. The fix for hallucination, if there is one, would require something architecturally novel — a module that tracks the *provenance* of representations (training-derived vs. inference-constructed), analogous to what the fusiform gyrus does with signal strength.

## The Deeper Asymmetry

The brain uses a *physical* proxy for origin: sensory input is reliably stronger (more neurons activated, faster, more direct) than imagination, which is generated by more diffuse top-down processes. Strength correlates with origin because the two pathways are anatomically different.

In a transformer, there's no such correlation. A confidently retrieved fact and a confident confabulation look identical in activation space. The attention mechanism doesn't distinguish "I'm looking this up" from "I'm making this up" — it just weights by learned relevance.

One speculative possibility: the mid-depth "abstraction phase" in transformer representations (see llm-topology-representation.md) might be where something like a weak version of this check could operate — distinguishing highly-consistent, widely-corroborated patterns from locally-coherent-but-sparse constructions. But this would need to be explicitly trained toward, not assumed to emerge.

## Open Questions

- Is there any training signal that could create origin-awareness in LLMs, or does the architecture fundamentally prevent it?
- The vivid-imagination-as-real finding suggests the brain's reality check is *accuracy-limited* — it can fail. What are the analogous failure modes of a hypothetical LLM origin-tracker?
- Does chain-of-thought reasoning create a partial proxy for the reality signal? Explicitly reasoning about "I remember X from training" vs "I'm inferring X" might approximate the circuit... very imperfectly.
