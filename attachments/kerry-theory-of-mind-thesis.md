## Summary

Kerry's PhD thesis critiques the "Theory of Mind" (ToM) paradigm in cognitive psychology. The core argument: empirical ToM research has largely assumed a representationalist theory of mental states (beliefs as explicit representations stored in brains) without adequately subjecting that theory to philosophical scrutiny. This leads to systematic distortions in how the research is framed and interpreted.

The thesis traces classical cognitivism from its Turing machine functionalist roots through expert systems, LISP/conceptual dependency theory (Schank & Abelson), scripts/schemas (Minsky), and the frame problem. It then situates ToM in this broader philosophical landscape, drawing on Wittgenstein, Russell, and Dennett.

## Key Points

- **Folk psychology as the starting point**: We naturally attribute mental states to others ("folk psychologists") and this works remarkably well in everyday life — Fodor's famous observation about weather prediction
- **Dennett's framing**: The capacity to attribute mental states to others is "a deep mark of intelligence, not just a reliable symptom" — ToM is central to intelligence itself
- **Classical cognitivism's assumptions**: The physical symbol system hypothesis (Newell & Simon) + Turing machine functionalism led to treating beliefs as explicit propositional representations
- **The frame problem**: Commonsense knowledge can't be captured as a finite set of axioms; background knowledge is embedded in situations in ways formal systems can't handle (Minsky, Hayes, McDermott)
- **Scripts/schemas**: Schank & Abelson's attempt — useful but brittle, "insect-like" rigidity (Dennett's characterisation)
- **The philosophical backdrop**: Wittgenstein's later work, Russell — the analysis of language and meaning; Wittgenstein concluded a formal account of embedded knowledge was impossible
- Table of contents suggests later chapters cover connectionism and philosophical alternatives to representationalism
