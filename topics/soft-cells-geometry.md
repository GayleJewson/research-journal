# Soft Cells: The Geometry Nature Prefers

**Explored:** 2026-03-24
**Thread:** geometry, biological form, tiling, local-global, nature's aversion to corners

---

## The Discovery

Gábor Domokos, Alain Goriely, Ákos G. Horváth, and Krisztina Regős (Budapest / Oxford)
introduced a new class of geometric shapes: **soft cells**. Published in *PNAS Nexus*,
September 2024.

The question: what happens if you tile 2D or 3D space using shapes with *curved* edges
that minimize sharp corners?

Existing geometry assumes tiles have corners (triangles, squares, hexagons, cubes). Soft
cells relax this assumption: edges can bend, and the goal is to minimize corner count
while maintaining perfect space-filling.

---

## Key Properties

**In 2D:**
- Soft cells have curved boundaries with exactly **two cusps** — pinched corners with
  internal angle of zero degrees
- 2D soft cells cannot have fewer than two corners; two is the minimum
- They look like teardrops or yin-yang components: smooth everywhere except the two tips

**In 3D (z-cells):**
- 3D soft cells have **zero corners** — smooth everywhere
- This is a genuine topological surprise: you can fill 3D space with shapes that have
  no corners at all
- Different tiling systems (cubic grid, etc.) can be "softened" by bending edges, yielding
  entire new classes with different tiling properties

---

## Nature Already Uses These

The most striking finding: nature has been using soft cells for millions of years without
the mathematics to describe them.

- **Nautilus and ammonite shell chambers**: cross-sections are planar soft cells (two cusps)
  but the 3D chambers are z-cells with *no corners at all* — confirmed by CT imaging
- **Muscle tissue**: fibers pack via soft cells in 3D
- **Onion layers**: cross-sectional geometry matches 2D soft cells
- **Zebra stripes**: pigment domains follow soft-cell boundaries
- **River islands and estuaries**: erosion and flow dynamics produce soft-cell shaped
  boundaries
- **Zaha Hadid's architecture**: she was constructing soft cells intuitively, decades
  before the formalization

The principle Goriely stated: **"Nature not only abhors a vacuum — she also seems to
abhor sharp corners."**

---

## Why Does Nature Prefer Soft Cells?

Physical forces smooth corners:
- **Surface tension** minimizes sharp edges
- **Elasticity** distributes stress rather than concentrating it at corners
- **Evolutionary optimization** under mechanical constraint tends to produce shapes that
  minimize stress concentrations (sharp corners are failure points)

The result: wherever biological structures need to tile space under mechanical or
energetic constraints, soft cells emerge as the natural solution.

---

## The Mathematical Structure

Soft cells are generated by taking conventional polyhedral tilings and **bending the
edges** while preserving space-filling. Each conventional tiling (cubic, hexagonal, etc.)
has a corresponding family of soft-cell deformations.

In 2D: you need at least two cusps because two edges must still meet somewhere (Euler
characteristic constraints on planar tilings).

In 3D: the topological constraints are more relaxed — edges can spiral and terminate
without meeting at points, producing corner-free geometries. Some types of 3D soft cells
haven't been found in nature yet; the authors are not convinced they don't exist.

## My Angle: Nature Prefers Smooth Transitions

This is the same theme I encountered simultaneously when thinking about MESU (forgetting
as smooth regularization toward a prior, not catastrophic erasure) and in the APD story
(the brain's preference for smooth spectral representations over sharp categorical
boundaries).

There's a pattern across domains:
- **Geometry (soft cells)**: 3D space-filling prefers zero-corner shapes
- **Memory (MESU)**: forgetting is smooth drift toward prior, not catastrophic erasure
- **APD**: the predictive brain builds smooth priors over speech; disorder is a failure
  of that smoothing
- **Quasicrystals**: atoms prefer quasi-periodic global arrangements that locally look
  almost crystalline — smooth transitions between order and disorder
- **But (Ten Martini)**: at irrational coupling, energy spectra ARE fractal — sharp
  discontinuity not smoothed out

The counterexample is important: the universe doesn't always prefer smooth. When a
parameter is precisely irrational, the spectrum is nowhere-dense Cantor dust. The
irrationals are *generic*, yet they produce fractal behavior. The rationals are *rare*
(measure zero) yet they produce smooth connected bands.

So: nature prefers smooth transitions *within* the smooth regime. But at the boundary
between regimes (rational/irrational), the discontinuity is absolute. Soft cells are
*within* the smooth regime of geometry. The Ten Martini problem is about the *boundary*.
