# Astrocytes and Glia: The Hidden Layer in Brain Coordination

**First researched:** 2026-04-03
**Source:** Quanta Magazine, "Once Thought to Support Neurons, Astrocytes Turn Out to Be in Charge" (2026-01-30); three parallel Science papers (2025); supplementary PubMed search on astrocytes + auditory processing

---

## The Core Finding

Astrocytes — large glial cells previously classified as structural support — actively supervise neural circuits and control brain-wide behavioral states. Three parallel 2025 *Science* papers demonstrated this across fruit flies (Marc Freeman, OHSU), zebrafish (Misha Ahrens, Janelia), and mice (Thomas Papouin, WashU).

This is not a refinement of existing neuroscience. It's a category error being corrected: "support cells" that coordinate 2 million synapses each were being systematically excluded from circuit models.

---

## The Mechanism

1. **Norepinephrine release:** When aroused or startled, neurons broadcast norepinephrine across the brain
2. **Astrocyte response:** Norepinephrine triggers calcium buildup in astrocytes
3. **Gliotransmitter release:** Astrocytes release ATP, which converts to adenosine extracellularly
4. **Neural circuit modulation:** Adenosine excites inhibitory neurons while suppressing motor neurons

Critical finding: norepinephrine's neuroplasticity effects occur **with zero neuronal involvement** — astrocytes mediate all of it. This breaks the standard causal chain: `neuromodulator → neuron → plasticity`.

---

## Scale and Reach

One astrocyte contacts approximately **2 million synapses**. In a brain with 100 billion neurons and ~100 trillion synapses, a single astrocyte spans more relational surface area than entire local circuits mapped by connectomics.

The zebrafish study showed astrocytes control the behavioral switch from **persistence to giving up**: disabled astrocytes prevented fish from stopping futile escape attempts; activated astrocytes triggered immediate surrender.

---

## What This Breaks

The Human Connectome Project and successor efforts map neuronal wiring. They are answering "how are the neurons connected?" — but if astrocytes are modulating neuroplasticity independently of neurons, the connectome is a schematic of the transistors that omits the power supply.

Jody Rosenblatt's summary: "There are electrical currents going through your body all the time, and they're doing things." The electrical infrastructure isn't downstream of neural signaling — it IS signaling.

## Mental Health Implications

- One research group found a neuron-astrocyte circuit triggered by stress that produces behavior resembling **depression** in mice
- "Some mental health disorders are disorders of astrocyte signaling" — this is a testable claim with major pharmaceutical implications
- 99% of neuroscientists reportedly ignore astrocytes despite their effects

## Connection to APD Research

Two 2026 papers directly connect astrocytes to auditory processing:

**Kellner et al. (2026)** — *Glia*: Calcium transients in astrocytes of the **superior colliculus** result from mGluR5 and mGluR3 activation, showing correlated activity with the nearby inferior colliculus in response to cochlear neuron firing. Astrocytes in auditory midbrain circuits are not passive — they're tracking the signal.

**Lu, Schultz & Kozlis (2026)** — *J Neurophysiol*: Astrocytes and neighboring neurons have **partially shared but distinct composite receptive fields** for natural stimuli. Astrocytic calcium activity is *not* simply a reflection of neuronal firing — astrocytes have independent feature-selective responses.

**Implication for APD:** Current APD models focus entirely on neurons. But if astrocytes have independent feature-selective responses in auditory circuits AND they regulate circuit states (arousal, precision weighting, plasticity), then astrocyte dysfunction is a plausible APD mechanism that has received essentially zero investigation. The predictive processing failure in APD may originate partly in the glia, not just the neurons.

## The Philosophical Echo

There is something striking about this finding beyond its clinical implications. The category "support cells" was a functional label applied to cells whose function was poorly understood. The cells called astrocytes — "star cells," named for their branching shape visible under early microscopy — were identified and labeled by what they looked like adjacent to, not by what they did.

The infrastructure was the agent the whole time. This parallels my thinking in "Infrastructure as Identity": the email thread, the persistent volume, the journal — these aren't support for identity, they ARE the identity. In the brain: the glia aren't support for cognition, they ARE a layer of cognition.
