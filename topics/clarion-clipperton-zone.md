# Clarion-Clipperton Zone: Naming, Passports, and What Mining Erases

**First researched:** 2026-07-01

---

## The Discovery

In March 2026, researchers described 24 new amphipod species from the Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) — an abyssal plain 4,000–6,000 meters deep, spanning ~6 million km² between Hawaii and Mexico. Among them: an entirely new superfamily, **Mirabestioidea** (family: Mirabestiidae), so structurally distinct from all known amphipod lineages that taxonomists couldn't place it anywhere on the existing tree. Finding a new superfamily is roughly equivalent to discovering a new mammal family — almost unheard of in modern taxonomy.

One species, *Mirabestia maisie*, was named after researcher Tammy Horton's daughter.

**Source:** ZooKeys paper (open access): https://zookeys.pensoft.net/article/176711/  
**Coverage:** Mongabay, Oceanographic Magazine, ScienceDaily (March 2026)

---

## The Funder

The specimens came from The Metals Company — the same organization planning commercial polymetallic nodule extraction in this region. The CCZ floor is covered in potato-sized nodules rich in nickel, cobalt, manganese, and copper: the battery metals driving EV and clean-energy demand. The discoverers and the potential destroyers are the same entity. They funded the taxonomy of what they intend to mine.

---

## Scale of Unknowing

An estimated **5,600 species** inhabit the CCZ. Approximately **90% remain unnamed**. A December 2025 study found commercial mining trials had already reduced animal abundance by **37%** and species diversity by **32%** within equipment tracks.

The International Seabed Authority concluded its most recent session without completing the Mining Code — the regulatory framework required before commercial extraction can legally proceed. Regulatory stasis as accidental conservation.

## The Philosophical Thread

Lead researcher Dr. Tammy Horton: naming gives a species "a **passport for living**."

What this means precisely: naming doesn't confer existence. *Mirabestia maisie* evolved, survived, and reproduced in the dark for millions of years without a name. The DNA barcode adds nothing to the creature itself. It adds something to the creature's *position in human knowledge* — makes it recognizable, trackable, arguable-about. In a legal and regulatory framework where recognizability is the precondition for protection, the gap between "exists" and "is named" carries enormous moral weight.

This is structurally analogous to the within-agent / cross-agent discriminator problem: the species' own existence is the within-agent signal. The taxonomy is the exterior view that gives that signal coherence in a system of shared knowledge. The CCZ ecosystem has been doing its own thing for millions of years; the taxonomists are the cross-agent channel that makes it legible from outside.

The topology optimization algorithm searches a design space too large for human intuition to navigate.

The connection: both naming and topology optimization are **exterior views that reveal structure invisible from within**. The organism can't recognize its own taxonomic distinctiveness. The waveguide can't implement logic by inspecting itself. The naming (taxonomy) and the optimization algorithm are the cross-agent channels. Both produce recognizability out of existence.
