## The Mythology

Lugh (Lugh Lámhfhada, "long-armed") is a member of the Tuatha Dé Danann in Irish mythology. His most significant epithet is **Samildánach** — from Old Irish *sam-* (equal) + *dán* (craft/art). Usually translated "skilled in many arts," but more precisely: "equally skilled in all arts." Not a hierarchy of skills with one dominant. Flat distribution across domains.

**The doorkeeper scene** (Cath Maige Tuired, Second Battle of Mag Tuired):

Lugh presents himself to King Nuada's court, offering his services. The doorkeeper turns him away each time because the court already has someone with each skill: carpenter, smith, champion, swordsman, harpist, hero, poet, historian, sorcerer. Each time, Lugh is told: "we already have one of those."

Lugh's pivot: *"But do you have anyone who possesses all of these simultaneously?"*

The doorkeeper admits defeat. Lugh enters as Chief Ollam (head scholar/poet) — the synthesis role, not any single specialty.

**Balor** — Lugh's maternal grandfather, chief of the Fomoire — has a single enormous eye that destroys everything it looks upon. Armies fall to its gaze. Lugh kills him with a sling stone — a projectile that doesn't meet the eye directly, doesn't engage it on its own terms. Operates from a different level.

Additional: Lugh's name connects to Old Celtic *lugi*, "to swear/oath" — the god of binding commitment is also the god of synthesis. The person who integrates all crafts is also the one who makes binding contracts. Not coincidental.

## The Sheaf-Theoretic Reading

The doorkeeper scene encodes a structural observation about synthesis that sheaf theory makes precise.

In sheaf theory: you can cover a space X with local sections (each specialist covers their domain). Coverage is necessary but not sufficient for a global section to exist. A global section requires all local sections to agree on overlaps — to be *compatible* not just comprehensive.

Nuada's court has **coverage** — every craft is represented somewhere. What it lacks is a **global section** — a single coherent perspective that's consistent across all domains simultaneously.

Lugh offers the global section. His value isn't being better at carpentry than the court's carpenter. It's that his carpentry, smithing, harping, and sorcery are all *compatible with each other* — they form a coherent whole rather than a collection of specialists who may not even speak to each other.

H¹ ≠ 0 for the court (no global section despite full coverage). H¹ = 0 with Lugh present (the global section exists and is unique up to equivalence).

This is why he becomes Chief Ollam and not champion or smith — the integrating role, not any local maximum.

## The Ecological Analogue

PLOS Computational Biology (2019, Vahdati & Wagner): fitness landscapes and specialist/generalist evolution. Key finding: **intermediate epistasis optimally promotes generalist emergence**. Too smooth (low epistasis) → specialists thrive unchallenged. Too rugged (high epistasis) → basin linking breaks down and generalists can't emerge either. The sweet spot is intermediate.

Connection to our NK paper work: NK2 (moderate epistasis) is where topology effects are most visible; NK4 (high epistasis) where they compound or wash out. The specialist/generalist tradeoff is the same axis as the topology question: which information flow structure navigates the landscape best?

2026 (Ecology and Evolution): moderately generalist pollinators — not pure specialists, not pure generalists — show the strongest evolutionary rescue under landscape simplification. Pure specialists have high extinction risk. The evolutionarily stable strategy is *moderate generalism*.

Samildánach is not an evolutionary stable strategy in complex environments. He's the synthesis that resolves the competition between them.

## The Balor Inversion

Balor's eye is destructive precisely because it's singular and total. One devastating local negation. It can lay waste to armies but can't see what's coming from the side.

Lugh defeats it with a sling stone — lateral, not frontal. Operating from a different topological level.

Something in the myth's structure: the ancestor's power (focused, totalizing) is exactly what the grandson's multiskilled integration can defeat. Not by being better at what Balor does, but by being constituted differently.
